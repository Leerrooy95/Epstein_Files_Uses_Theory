## Executive Summary

In early 2026, Venezuela's transitional leadership enacted two significant reforms: opening the oil sector to foreign operators and issuing a limited amnesty for political offenses. While presented as humanitarian and market-friendly measures, the actual "guarantees" for investors and political actors operate through U.S. Treasury control and discretionary enforcement rather than statutory protection. This reset serves multiple functions: stabilizing investor confidence, enabling elite survival, and appearing to address political reconciliation while maintaining state leverage over justice outcomes.

## 1. Hydrocarbons Law Reform: U.S. Custody as the Real Guarantee

**Legal Act:** Law Amending the Organic Hydrocarbons Law  
**Signed:** January 29, 2026, by Acting President Delcy Rodríguez

### Key Provisions

- Ends PDVSA's (state oil company) monopoly on oil production
- Permits full private and foreign investment in hydrocarbon operations
- Establishes royalty rates capped at 30%, favoring investors
- **Arbitration Status:** The law permits arbitration clauses in contracts, subject to Ministry approval, but does *not* mandate independent international arbitration as a statutory right. Arbitration is therefore contractually allowed but not institutionally guaranteed as a default for all investors.

### The Guarantee: Operational Control, Not Legal Protection

Early media and legal analyses speculated that mandatory international arbitration would provide statutory protection for foreign investors. This was ultimately clarified: **the primary risk mitigation now rests on U.S. General License 46**.

> **The United States Treasury controls the marketing and custodial disbursement of Venezuelan oil revenues.**

Foreign companies operating in Venezuela depend on U.S. Treasury approval under General License 46 for receipt and distribution of payment, providing a *de facto* guarantee that supersedes Venezuelan legal authority. This operational control is considerably more robust for investors than any domestic statutory clause, which remains subject to Venezuelan ministerial discretion and potential revocation.

### Why This Matters

This arrangement represents the transition from state sovereignty to operational subordination. Venezuela can pass investment-friendly laws and contractually offer arbitration provisions, but oil revenue flows—and, by extension, investor payment security—are dependent on ongoing U.S. regulatory approval, not on Venezuelan legal protections. 

The "legal reset" is thus functionally a shift to external, operational control mechanisms. Investors have greater security from U.S. Treasury licensing authority than from Venezuelan statutory guarantees because:
- U.S. General License 46 operates independently of Venezuelan government decisions
- Revenue streams are custodied outside Venezuelan sovereignty
- U.S. regulatory frameworks supersede Venezuelan ministerial authority

**Sources:** PBS NewsHour (Jan 29), Sky News (Jan 30), Holland & Knight Legal Alert (Feb 4), Stratfor

## 2. Amnesty Law for Democratic Coexistence: Selective Relief with Structural Ambiguity

**Timeline:** Announced January 30, 2026; debated and passed February 5–6, 2026

### Scope and Exclusions

The amnesty law covers most political and protest-related offenses committed since 1999, including:
- Political activism and organization
- Protest participation
- Civil disobedience related to political grievances

**Explicit Exclusions:** The law does not apply to:
- Killings (homicides)
- Serious human rights violations
- Drug trafficking offenses

### The Legal Ambiguity

By separating "killings" from "serious human rights violations," the law creates discretionary classification power. Authorities can now argue that:
- Security force lethal action during dispersals = "public order operation" (protected under amnesty)
- OR same action = "homicide" or "human rights violation" (prosecutable)

The distinction enables selective justice and continued leverage over both security apparatus and dissidents.

### Prisoner Release Statistics

As of February 2, 2026:

| Metric | Count | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Released since Jan 8 | 302 verified | Stratfor, NGO reports |
| Estimated remaining | ~711 | NGO estimates |
| Initial UN verification | ~50 (mid-January) | UN fact-finding |

**Important Note:** The discrepancy between initial UN counts (~50) and later releases (302+) suggests either administrative lag, number inflation in official statements, or evolving definitional standards for "political prisoner." The partial nature of the amnesty is thus demonstrated by both the absolute numbers and the retention of substantial political detention.

### Humanitarian Function and Limits

While the amnesty enables the release of hundreds of political prisoners and projects an image of national reconciliation, it simultaneously preserves the state's ability to:
- Continue detaining prisoners classified outside amnesty scope
- Maintain prosecutorial discretion over security forces
- Retain institutional memory and operational continuity

**Sources:** AP News (Jan 30), Havana Times (Jan 30), Fox News (Jan 31), Stratfor, NGO and UN reporting

## 3. Maria Corina Machado: Opposition Leader as Legitimacy Asset

### Background

Maria Corina Machado, a prominent opposition figure, was awarded the **2025 Nobel Peace Prize** on October 10, 2025. The Nobel Committee cited her "tireless work promoting democratic rights" and notably, her choice to remain in Venezuela despite legal threats and harassment.

### Amnesty Application

The February 2026 amnesty law cleared the charges that had forced her into hiding, allowing her public re-emergence rather than literal release from detention.

### Political Significance

Her return as an internationally-recognized Nobel laureate serves multiple functions:
- Provides democratic legitimacy to the transitional government
- Signals that opposition voices are now safe/welcomed
- Offers international backing for the new regime
- Demonstrates the amnesty's "generosity" to external observers

This illustrates how amnesty laws function beyond justice: they are instruments of political positioning and international narrative management.

**Sources:** Nobel Committee (Oct 10, 2025), AP News, Caracas Chronicles

## 4. El Helicoide Closure: Symbolic Reform Under External Pressure

### Timeline and Announcement

| Date | Event | Catalyst |
|------|-------|---------|
| Jan 6, 2026 | U.S. President Trump publicly identifies El Helicoide as a "torture chamber" and calls for closure | External pressure |
| Jan 30, 2026 | Interim President Delcy Rodríguez signs official closure order | Response to pressure |

### The Facility and Its History

El Helicoide was Venezuela's primary political detention and torture center. Its closure represents a visible human rights concession and removal of a symbol associated with the Chavista regime.

### Conversion Purpose

The facility will be converted into a "sports, social, and cultural center" specifically designated for police families and neighboring communities.

### Critical Observation

While the closure symbolically rejects the previous regime's brutality, the designation for police families rather than as a memorial or transitional justice site suggests:
- Symbolic erasure of torture history
- Recycling of infrastructure back to the security apparatus that staffed it
- Absence of victim-centered remembrance or accountability mechanisms

**Sources:** The Guardian (Feb 5), AP News (Jan 30), public statements from transitional government

## 5. Jorge Rodríguez and Administrative Continuity

### Public Position on Amnesty

Jorge Rodríguez, a key figure in both Chavista and transitional governance, has publicly stated that the amnesty covers political offenses "since 1999" while explicitly excluding "killings, serious human rights abuses, and drug trafficking."

### What Is NOT Being Said

Notably absent from his statements is any confession, apology, or acknowledgment of personal or institutional responsibility for state violence. The framing is pragmatic ("we must move forward") rather than confessional.

### Strategic Function

This positioning allows:
- The Rodríguez family and associated elites to survive regime transition
- Retention of institutional knowledge and operational capacity within the state
- Presentation of "pragmatic peacebuilding" rather than accountability
- Continuation of selective enforcement power

This exemplifies administrative continuity masked by transitional rhetoric—a consistent pattern across regime changes where functional elites rebrand rather than face consequences.
